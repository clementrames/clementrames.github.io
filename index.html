Society-In-The-Loop - emerging behavioral insights shaping urban design

This project examines the impacts of emerging digitally-enabled mobility technologies and services. Access to data beyond just end-users is explored, specifically in terms of how and where mobility choices and service providers shape broader energy, public health, environmental and equity outcomes. This question is addressed through a case study of three districts in the Denver, Colorado region, and informed by urban design approaches from other global cities. Key findings include the extent to which the use of new mobility technologies and services inform mobility decisions (individual), optimal system operation (society) and spatial-temporal dynamics of shared mobility services. The proposed design principles build on human-centered and place-based data analyses to inform future synergies between shared, electric and automated mobility:
- Society-in-the-loop: incorporate diverse human values, beliefs and norms in built environment, mobility and institutional systems design, fostering the co-evolution of technology and society
- Participatory urban design: steer new mobility services towards wider societal co-benefits
- Multi-directional information flow: leverage new digital communication channels to identify nudges to support sustainable mobility behaviors and data-driven decisions for urban planning
- Proactive risk analysis: examine unintended or adverse effects of new technologies and services – adjust and further optimize practices through a continuous user feedback loop.

  • People: Andrew Duvall, Joshua Sperling
  • Year: 2017

Mobility Decision Science

human behavior, urban form and shared mobility

Mobility Decision Science - human behavior, urban form and shared mobility

This effort aims to answer the following questions: to what extent can shared mobility help invert the trend of increasing Single Occupant Vehicle (SOV) trips? What are the energy risks and benefits of shared mobility? How do interactions between technology, policy, urban design and behavioral change shape the transition to energy-efficient transportation? To this end, a multi-criteria assessment framework for sustainable urban mobility is developed, incorporating metrics such as modal share, daily VMT, car ownership and population density. We apply this framework to three cities – Denver, CO, San Francisco, CA and Paris, France – to evaluate the sustainability of their transportation system and explore their potential for shared mobility. Relative energy savings from car-pooling, car-sharing, ride-hailing, electric, connected and automated vehicles are also compared.
The results from these 3 cities show strikingly different profiles: SOV trips vary from 70% in Denver to 30% in San Francisco and 17% in Paris, while transit ridership is 7%, 25% and 64% of trips respectively. These figures seem to correlate strongly with population density and degree of mixed-use development, with a tenfold increase in density from Denver to Paris. Factors such as urban governance structures and level of public transit service also vary widely across cities, and further help to explain the observed differences. [Behavior, Energy and Climate Change Conference Presentation]

  • People: Alejandro Henao, Andrew Duvall, Jacob Ward, Anand Gopal

insights from real-world spatial data mining

Electrifying Shared Mobility - insights from real-world spatial data mining

The urban mobility landscape has changed drastically in the past five years with the emergence of Transportation Network Companies (TNCs) providing app-based ride-hailing services. The rapid growth of these services and their impacts on traffic congestion and transit ridership are highly controversial. RideAustin (a non-profit TNC) has published an open source dataset consisting of 1.5 million rides completed by almost 5,000 drivers over a 10-month period (between June 2016 and April 2017) in the Austin, TX area. The vehicles used by RideAustin drivers were compared to 2016 registration data for Austin, and show on average more recent vehicles and a higher preference for small sedans, with only 0.5% EVs.
Data on non-passenger trips – i.e. commuting from and to the driver’s residence and ‘dead-heading’ trips between drop-offs and pick-ups – were unavailable. These trips were reconstructed by chaining consecutive drop-offs and pick-ups, resulting in conservative estimates of empty vehicle miles driven. A key finding from the exploration of this dataset is the “efficiency ratio”, as defined by passenger miles divided by all miles driven, of 51%. While 21% of all shifts exceed 100 miles, only 2.6% exceed 200 miles, which indicates that, as longer range EVs become available, EVs become better suited to ride-hailing operations.
[forthcoming SAE World Congress paper: Analysis of Fast Charging Station Network for Electrified Ride-Hailing Services]

  • People: Eleftheria Kontou, Eric Wood, Andrew Meintz, John Smart
  • Year: 2017

National PEV Infrastructure Roadmap

National PEV Infrastructure Roadmap - fast charging long distance travel

The EERE National PEV Infrastructure Roadmap, shows what effective co-evolution of the EV fleet and charging infrastructure might look like under a range of scenarios. Advanced GIS analysis was used to maximize infrastructure’s economic viability while enabling efficient network growth as the EV market matures.
The potential number, capacity, and location of charging stations needed to enable broad EV adoption over the coming decades hinge on not only technological variables, but also consumer preferences and driving behaviors. Results suggest that relatively few corridor DCFC stations could enable long-distance EV travel between U.S. cities. Urban communities, however, are expected to have significantly larger charging infrastructure requirements both to provide coverage and support demand in high EV adoption.
[DOE Fact of the Week]

  • People: Eric Wood, Matteo Muratori, Sesha Raghavan, Marc Melaina
  • Year: 2017

Smart Columbus

jump-starting electric vehicles in smart cities

Smart Columbus - jump-starting electric vehicles in smart cities

This project estimated the Electric Vehicle (EV) charging infrastructure needs in Columbus, OH, winner of DOT’s Smart Cities award, to help drive its vision of greater equity, sustainability and accessibility forward. Acknowledging that “Smart is just the start”, I leveraged a 1.6 billion waypoint GPS dataset collected from cellular devices to locate charging stations where they will best serve citizens’ needs.
A regional land use dataset was processed to infer workplace and multi-unit residential locations, which are prime candidates for shared charging infrastructure. I co-developed the Electric Vehicle Infrastructure Projection tool to anticipate EV charging demand, and validated simulation results with real world station utilization data from charging provider ChargePoint. [forthcoming NREL report]
[forthcoming ITEC Conference paper: Development of a DC Fast Charging Station Model for Use with EV Infrastructure Projection Tool]

HyperLoop Sustainability

beyond the hype: environment, economics and equity

HyperLoop Sustainability - beyond the hype: environment, economics and equity

The HyperLoop, a novel concept set to disrupt long-distance travel, consists of pods propelled in an evacuated tube by linear accelerators, gliding at transonic speeds, and powered by solar energy. The HyperLoop aims to be safer, faster, cheaper, and more sustainable than other current modes of travel. As such, is an extremely attractive idea, challenging the status quo by introducing a novel self-powered mode of transportation.
A quantitative model to assess the environmental, economic, and social sustainability of the HyperLoop was developed. A whole life-cycle analysis of materials and processes was performed. The impacts on both natural ecosystems and urban dynamics were investigated. Based on the results of the model, concrete suggestions for sustainable implementation of this concept were proposed.

  • Year: 2016

Torque Estimation in Automotive Powertrain - adaptive control systems for tomorrow's engines

Engine torque is a key variable required by modern vehicle control systems to improve performance and efficiency. Typical measurement of torque is achieved through additional sensors, however their cost and form factor make them unsuitable for production engines. The aim of this project was to apply estimation methods to infer the torque acting on various key stages of the powertrain. A holistic powertrain model was derived from physical principles and offers realistic results in simulation. Input and parameter estimation techniques were investigated. Validation of the estimation techniques was performed on an experimental test bench that represents a scaled-down version of a vehicle drivetrain. This novel method of powertrain torque estimation was shown to be stable and effective in simulation, demonstrating commercial potential. The experimental rig demonstrated the real-world applicability of our estimation techniques. This project was awarded the best project award from the UK's Institution of Mechanical Engineers.
[IFAC Conference paper: Engine torque estimation with integrated unknown input observer and adaptive parameter estimator]
[UKACC Conference paper: Air-fuel-ratio control of engine system with unknown input observer]

Silicon Audio

bio-inspired design for disruptive microphones and seismometers

Silicon Audio - bio-inspired design for disruptive microphones and seismometers

Took a disruptive piezoelectric microfabricated microphone design from concept stage to finished product ready for commercialization in smart watches and hearing assistive devices. This directional microphone is biologically inspired by the Ormia Ochracea fly’s hearing organ, which resembles a microscopic teeter-totter rocking about a central pivot in response to minute pressure differences as sound waves pass across the structure. Performed acoustics and microelectronics research, analytical modeling, Finite Element Analysis, CAD modeling, prototype fabrication, and extensive lab and field testing to fulfill sometimes conflicting customer requirements. Modeled the temperature drift of an ultralow noise optical seismometer, designed an experimental rig and executed a rigorous test procedure to validate the model and obtain statistically relevant temperature drift data.

Acoustics of Bubbly Liquids

bubbles that change the speed of sound

Acoustics of Bubbly Liquids - bubbles that change the speed of sound

Several medical techniques have been developed that make use of the special acoustic behavior of bubbles. Ultrasound imaging uses microbubbles as contrast agents to facilitate diagnosis, while high intensity focused ultrasound relies on bubbles' resonance to induce hemostasis. Even the quality of Champagne is measured by the size of its bubbles. To enable further insight into the nonlinear acoustic properties of bubbly liquids, a mobile demonstration setup was built. Air bubbles are injected into a water-filled cylindrical PVC pipe via 12 needles. The air flow rate, and hence void fraction, is varied with a valve. A hydrophone was designed and built by soldering leads to a piezoelectric ceramic disk, coupled with a custom-made preamplifier based on an AD797 op-amp chip. The amplified hydrophone signal is routed to speakers as well as a frequency analyzer displaying the mode frequencies of the pipe.

  • People: Preston Wilson, Gabriel Venegas
  • Year: 2015
