Urban Science and Shared Mobility

Society-In-The-Loop

emerging behavioral insights shaping urban design

Society-In-The-Loop - emerging behavioral insights shaping urban design

This study examines the impacts of emerging digitally-enabled mobility technologies and services. Access to data beyond just end-users is explored, specifically in terms of how and where mobility choices and service providers shape broader energy, public health, environmental and equity outcomes. Two primary research aims are addressed through a case study of three districts in the Denver, Colorado region, and informed by urban design approaches from other global cities. First, we investigate the extent to which new smartphone & web-based mobility services shape uni-, bi- and multi-directional information flows. Second, we identify the potential for increased effectiveness in shaping mobility decisions by proposing four design principles, using evidence from the literature and data collected.
Key findings include the extent to which the use of new mobility technologies and services inform mobility decisions (individual), optimal system operation (society) and spatial-temporal dynamics of shared mobility services. The below design principles build on human-centered and place-based data analyses to inform future synergies between shared, electric and automated mobility:
- Society-in-the-loop: incorporate diverse human values, beliefs and norms in built environment, mobility and institutional systems design, fostering the co-evolution of technology and society
- Participatory urban design: steer new mobility services towards wider societal co-benefits
- Multi-directional information flow: leverage new digital communication channels to identify nudges to support sustainable mobility behaviors and data-driven decisions for urban planning
- Proactive risk analysis: examine unintended or adverse effects of new technologies and services – adjust and further optimize practices through a continuous user feedback loop.

  • People: Andrew Duvall, Joshua Sperling
  • Year: 2017

Mobility Decision Science

human behavior, urban form and shared mobility

Mobility Decision Science - human behavior, urban form and shared mobility

Evaluating the impacts of ride-hailing on car ownership using a propensity score weighted regression analysis with Uber and Lyft entry dates and vehicle registrations at the ZIP code level. Characterizing ‘deadheading’ (distance driven cruising around while waiting for rides) and ‘overheading’ (distance driven from dispatch to pick up location) using 1.5 million ride-hailing trips dataset from non-profit, local ride-hailing service RideAustin.
Examining the relationship between mobility behavior and urban form, using metrics such as modal share, daily VMT, car ownership and population density. Comparing the relative energy savings from car-pooling, car-sharing, ride-hailing, electric, connected and automated vehicles.
[Behavior, Energy and Climate Change Conference Presentation]

  • People: Andrew Duvall, Alejandro Henao, Jacob Ward, Anand Gopal
  • Year: 2017

Advanced Fueling Infrastructure

electrifying high mileage shared fleets

Advanced Fueling Infrastructure - electrifying high mileage shared fleets

Ride-hailing data synthesis from real-world GPS travel dataset. Matched rides to vehicles based on proximity in time and space - reducing vehicles, but adding miles to the system (empty vehicle deadheading). Then simulated this pseudo-synthetic dataset in EV driving/charging model (EVI-Pro) to anticipate infrastructure needs for a fleet of electrified ride-hailing vehicles. DC Fast Charging (DCFC, >50kW) demand projected to double, and motivated a deeper study of DCFC station utilization and financial viability.
[forthcoming SAE World Congress paper: Analysis of Fast Charging Station Network for Electrified Ride-Hailing Services]
High capital costs and uneven power demand have been major challenges to the widespread deployment of DCFC stations. Developed a DCFC station load model based on Monte Carlo simulations to predict the charging load and queuing time at three stations – in urban, suburban and rural settings. Proposed a charging control strategy to reduce operational costs while minimizing impacts on Quality of Service.
[forthcoming ITEC Conference paper: Development of a DC Fast Charging Station Model for Use with EV Infrastructure Projection Tool]

  • People: Eleftheria Kontou, Eric Wood, Andrew Meintz, John Smart
  • Year: 2017

National PEV Infrastructure Roadmap

National PEV Infrastructure Roadmap - fast charging long distance travel

Created innovative data visualizations and performed advanced GIS analysis for the EERE National PEV Infrastructure Roadmap, planning a network of chargers to maximize their economic viability while enabling efficient network growth as the PEV market matures. This analysis shows what effective co-evolution of the PEV fleet and charging infrastructure might look like under a range of scenarios. The potential number, capacity, and location of charging stations needed to enable broad PEV adoption over the coming decades hinge on not only technological variables, but also consumer preferences and driving behaviors. [DOE Fact of the Week]

Smart Columbus

Smart Columbus - jump-starting electric vehicles in smart cities

Estimated EV charging infrastructure needs in Columbus, OH, winner of DOT’s Smart Cities award, leveraging a combination of a 1.6 billion waypoints GPS travel dataset, a high resolution land use dataset, and real-world charging station utilization data.
Processed 1.6 bn waypoint GPS dataset to analyze travel patterns in the region. Built EVI-Pro simulation tool to anticipate charging requirements. Combined land use information with GPS data to infer residential and workplace locations for EV infrastructure planning. Validated simulation results with real world station utilization data from charging provider ChargePoint. [link to report]

HyperLoop Sustainability

beyond the hype: environment, economics and equity

HyperLoop Sustainability - beyond the hype: environment, economics and equity

The HyperLoop, a novel concept set to disrupt long-distance travel, consists of pods propelled in an evacuated tube by linear accelerators, and gliding at transonic speeds. The tube would be covered by a solar photovoltaic array which, combined with battery storage, would power the whole system. The main claims of the HyperLoop is that it would be safer, faster, cheaper, and more sustainable than other current modes of transportation. The change towards a sustainable world cannot happen through incremental improvements, but rather through radical innovation. Thus, the HyperLoop appears an extremely attractive idea, as it aims to challenge the status quo by introducing a novel self-powered mode of transportation. A quantitative model to assess the environmental, economic, and social sustainability of the HyperLoop is developed. Based on the results of the model, concrete suggestions for potential improvement are proposed.

  • People: Mohammed Wanous, Gokhul Ramakrishnan, Verner Viisainen
  • Year: 2016

Torque Estimation in Automotive Powertrain

adaptive control systems for tomorrow's engines

Torque Estimation in Automotive Powertrain - adaptive control systems for tomorrow's engines

Engine torque is a key variable required by modern vehicle control systems to improve performance and efficiency. Typical measurement of torque is achieved through additional sensors, however their cost and form factor make them unsuitable for production engines. The aim of this project was to apply estimation methods to infer the torque acting on various key stages of the powertrain. A holistic powertrain model was derived from physical principles and offers realistic results in simulation. Input and parameter estimation techniques were investigated. Validation of the estimation techniques was performed on an experimental test bench that represents a scaled-down version of a vehicle drivetrain. This novel method of powertrain torque estimation was shown to be stable and effective in simulation, demonstrating commercial potential. The experimental rig demonstrated the real-world applicability of our estimation techniques. This project was awarded the best project award from the UK's Institution of Mechanical Engineers.
[IFAC Conference paper: Engine torque estimation with integrated unknown input observer and adaptive parameter estimator]
[UKACC Conference paper: Air-fuel-ratio control of engine system with unknown input observer]

  • People: Guido Herrmann, Jing Na, Thomas Harding, Huang Yu Teh, Tobias Mill
  • Year: 2016

Silicon Audio

bio-inspired design for disruptive microphones and seismometers

Silicon Audio - bio-inspired design for disruptive microphones and seismometers

Took a disruptive piezoelectric microfabricated microphone design from concept stage to finished product ready for commercialization in smart watches and hearing assistive devices. This directional microphone is biologically inspired by the Ormia Ochracea fly’s hearing organ, which resembles a microscopic teeter-totter rocking about a central pivot in response to minute pressure differences as sound waves pass across the structure. Performed acoustics and microelectronics research, analytical modeling, Finite Element Analysis, CAD modeling, prototype fabrication, and extensive lab and field testing to fulfill sometimes conflicting customer requirements. Modeled the temperature drift of an ultralow noise optical seismometer, designed an experimental rig and executed a rigorous test procedure to validate the model and obtain statistically relevant temperature drift data.

  • Year: 2015

Acoustics of Bubbly Liquids

bubbles that change the speed of sound

Acoustics of Bubbly Liquids - bubbles that change the speed of sound

Several medical techniques have been developed that make use of the special acoustic behavior of bubbles. Ultrasound imaging uses microbubbles as contrast agents to facilitate diagnosis, while high intensity focused ultrasound relies on bubbles' resonance to induce hemostasis. Even the quality of Champagne is measured by the size of its bubbles. To enable further insight into the nonlinear acoustic properties of bubbly liquids, a mobile demonstration setup was built. Air bubbles are injected into a water-filled cylindrical PVC pipe via 12 needles. The air flow rate, and hence void fraction, is varied with a valve. A hydrophone was designed and built by soldering leads to a piezoelectric ceramic disk, couple with a custom-made preamplifier based on an AD797 op-amp chip. The amplified hydrophone signal is routed to speakers as well as a frequency analyzer displaying the mode frequencies of the pipe.

  • People: Preston Wilson, Gabriel Venegas
  • Year: 2015
