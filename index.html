Society-In-The-Loop - emerging behavioral insights shaping urban design

This study examines the impacts of emerging digitally enabled mobility technologies and services. It extends thinking beyond just end-users to a systems perspective where individuals’ mobility choices affect energy, public health, environmental and equity outcomes. Two primary research questions are addressed through a case study of three districts in the Denver, CO metropolitan area, and informed by urban design approaches from other global cities. First, we explore the extent to which new smartphone & web-based mobility services shape uni-, bi- and multi-directional information flows. Second, we identify the potential increased effectiveness in shaping mobility decisions by proposing four design principles, using evidence from the literature and data collected.
We focus on 3 distinct residential, commercial and mixed-use locations to understand resident and employee mobility choices from available options. The following technologies are investigated. Mobility-on-demand apps, which have radically transformed the urban mobility landscape in the past few years, collect some invaluable data to characterize user travel patterns. Often overlooked but perhaps even more instrumental are trip planning mobile and web apps, which have been shaping mobility decisions and now incorporate crowdsourced real-time information. At the time of writing, multi-modal integrated payments apps are not widely available, yet will be a crucial step in enabling large-scale adoption of shared mobility services.
Key findings include the extent to which the use of apps inform mobility decisions (individual), optimal system operation (society) and spatial-temporal dynamics of shared mobility services. The proposed design principles will build on initial human-centered and place-based data analyses to help inform future synergies between shared, electric and automated mobility:
- Society-in-the-loop: incorporate diverse human values, beliefs and norms in technology design
- Participatory design for co-benefits across social-ecological-economic-health-governance systems and services
- Leverage new digital communication channels for multi-directional information flow: sustainable mobility behavioral change and data-driven decision making for urban planning
- Proactively identify risks and unintended adverse effects of these technologies – correct imbalances through policy and a continuous citizen feedback loop
[Publication forthcoming.]

Mobility Decision Science - human behavior, urban form and shared mobility

Evaluating the impacts of ride-hailing on car ownership using a propensity score weighted regression analysis with Uber and Lyft entry dates and vehicle registrations at the ZIP code level. Characterizing ‘deadheading’ (distance driven cruising around while waiting for rides) and ‘overheading’ (distance driven from dispatch to pick up location) using 1.5 million ride-hailing trips dataset from non-profit, local ride-hailing service RideAustin . [Publications forthcoming.]
Examining the relationship between mobility behavior and urban form, using metrics such as modal share, daily VMT, car ownership and population density. Comparing the relative energy savings from car-pooling, car-sharing, ride-hailing, electric, connected and automated vehicles.
[Behavior, Energy and Climate Change Conference Presentation]

Advanced Fueling Infrastructure

electrifying high mileage shared fleets

Advanced Fueling Infrastructure - electrifying high mileage, shared fleets

Ride-hailing data synthesis from real-world GPS travel dataset. Matched rides to vehicles based on proximity in time and space - reducing vehicles, but adding miles to the system (empty vehicle deadheading). Then simulated this pseudo-synthetic dataset in EV driving/charging model (EVI-Pro) to anticipate infrastructure needs for a fleet of electrified ride-hailing vehicles. DC Fast Charging (DCFC, >50kW) demand projected to double, and motivated a deeper study of DCFC station utilization and financial viability. [SAE paper forthcoming.]
High capital costs and uneven power demand have been major challenges to the widespread deployment of DCFC stations. Developed a DCFC station load model based on Monte Carlo simulations to predict the charging load and queuing time at three stations – in urban, suburban and rural settings. Proposed a charging control strategy to reduce operational costs while minimizing impacts on Quality of Service. [ITEC paper forthcoming.]

HyperLoop Sustainability

beyond the hype: environment, economics and equity

HyperLoop Sustainability - beyond the hype: environment, economics and equity

The HyperLoop, a novel concept set to disrupt long-distance travel, consists of pods propelled in an evacuated tube by linear accelerators, and gliding at transonic speeds. The tube would be covered by a solar photovoltaic array which, combined with battery storage, would power the whole system. The main claims of the HyperLoop is that it would be safer, faster, cheaper, and more sustainable than other current modes of transportation. The change towards a sustainable world cannot happen through incremental improvements, but rather through radical innovation. Thus, the HyperLoop appears an extremely attractive idea, as it aims to challenge the status quo by introducing a novel self-powered mode of transportation. A quantitative model to assess the environmental, economic, and social sustainability of the HyperLoop. Based on the results of the model, concrete suggestions for potential improvement are proposed.

  • Year: 2016

Torque Estimation in Automotive Powertrain - adaptive control systems for tomorrow's engines

Engine torque is a key variable required by modern vehicle control systems to improve performance and efficiency. Typical measurement of torque is achieved through additional sensors, however their cost and form factor make them unsuitable for production engines. The aim of this project was to apply estimation methods to infer the torque acting on various key stages of the powertrain. A holistic powertrain model was derived from physical principles and offers realistic results in simulation. Input and parameter estimation techniques were investigated. Validation of the estimation techniques was performed on an experimental test bench that represents a scaled-down version of a vehicle drivetrain. This novel method of powertrain torque estimation was shown to be stable and effective in simulation, demonstrating commercial potential. The experimental rig demonstrated the real-world applicability of our estimation techniques. This project was awarded the best project award from the UK's Institution of Mechanical Engineers.

  • People: Guido Herrmann, Jing Na, Thomas Harding, Huang Yu Teh, Tobias Mill
  • Year: 2016

Silicon Audio

bio-inspired design for disruptive microphones and seismometers

Silicon Audio - bio-inspired design for disruptive microphones and seismometers

Took a disruptive piezoelectric microfabricated microphone design from concept stage to finished product ready for commercialization in smart watches and hearing assistive devices. This directional microphone is biologically inspired by the Ormia Ochracea fly’s hearing organ, which resembles a microscopic teeter-totter rocking about a central pivot in response to minute pressure differences as sound waves pass across the structure. Performed acoustics and microelectronics research, analytical modeling, Finite Element Analysis, CAD modeling, prototype fabrication, and extensive lab and field testing to fulfill sometimes conflicting customer requirements. Modeled the temperature drift of an ultralow noise optical seismometer, designed an experimental rig and executed a rigorous test procedure to validate the model and obtain statistically relevant temperature drift data.

  • Year: 2015

Acoustics of Bubbly Liquids

bubbles that change the speed of sound

Acoustics of Bubbly Liquids - bubbles that change the speed of sound

Several medical techniques have been developed that make use of the special acoustic behavior of bubbles. Ultrasound imaging uses microbubbles as contrast agents to facilitate diagnosis, while high intensity focused ultrasound relies on bubbles' resonance to induce hemostasis. Even the quality of Champagne is measured by the size of its bubbles. To enable further insight into the nonlinear acoustic properties of bubbly liquids, a mobile demonstration setup was built. Air bubbles are injected into a water-filled cylindrical PVC pipe via 12 needles. The air flow rate, and hence void fraction, is varied with a valve. A hydrophone was designed and built by soldering leads to a piezoelectric ceramic disk, couple with a custom-made preamplifier based on an AD797 op-amp chip. The amplified hydrophone signal is routed to speakers as well as a frequency analyzer displaying the mode frequencies of the pipe.
